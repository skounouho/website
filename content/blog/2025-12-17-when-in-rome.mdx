> As they [Romulus and Remus] were twins and no claim to precedence could be based on seniority, they decided to consult the tutelary deities of the place by means of augury as to who was to give his name to the new city, and who was to rule it after it had been founded. Romulus accordingly selected the Palatine as his station for observation, Remus the Aventine. - Livy, Ab Urbe Condita

Five years ago, I sat in a fluorescent-lit classroom translating the words above, being sure to parse tenses and antecedents correctly. Livy’s Ab Urbe Condita, which translates to “Concerning the founding of the city,” chronicles a series of notable stories and people in Rome’s history. The tales of conquest, piety, and civicism ignited my passion to study Latin and Roman history, and provided an inspiration for my own values. But the course itself was an academic exercise, where Romulus and Remus and their fraternal fights of the hills of Rome were tales.

Today, I stood on these hills.

To say it is surreal is an understatement. I walked through the arches of the Colosseum, along the crumbling colonnades of the Forum, gazed from the Aventine hill over the Circus Maximus toward the Palatine hill. Today is just the start of what I am sure will be a great adventure in Rome.
